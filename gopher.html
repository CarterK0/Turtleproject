Gopher Tortoise: Keystone Species of Alabama's Coastal Plain

Keystone Species: Gopherus polyphemus

Species Overview & Conservation Status

The Gopher Tortoise is the only native tortoise species east of the Mississippi River. They are known as a keystone species because their deep burrows provide critical refuge for over 360 other animal species (commensals), including the federally threatened Eastern Indigo Snake. Without the tortoise, these other species would struggle to survive in the ecosystem.

  • Size: Adults typically measure 9 to 11 inches in length.
  • Lifespan: Can live 40-60 years in the wild.
  • Shell Color: Tan, brown, or gray.
  • Alabama Status: Populations west of the Mobile and Tombigbee Rivers (Choctaw, Washington, and Mobile counties) are Federally Threatened. In the rest of its Alabama range, it is protected by state law as a non-game species of High Conservation Concern.
  • Primary Threat: Habitat loss and fragmentation, largely due to development and the suppression of natural, necessary fires in the longleaf pine ecosystem.

The gopher tortoise is known for its domed shell and shovel-like front limbs used for digging.

Habitat & Range in Alabama

Gopher tortoises thrive in the dry, sandy upland habitats of the Coastal Plain that historically supported the Longleaf Pine ecosystem. These conditions are necessary for them to dig their extensive burrows and for their eggs to incubate successfully.

Ideal Alabama Habitat:

They prefer Longleaf Pine-Scrub Oak-Wiregrass Sand Hills. The key features are:

  • Soil: Dry, deep, well-drained sandy soils for burrow excavation.
  • Canopy: Open canopy cover to allow sufficient sunlight for thermoregulation, nesting, and the growth of low herbaceous forage plants (grasses, forbs, legumes).
  • Maintenance: Habitats must be maintained by frequent prescribed fire to prevent canopy closure and encourage groundcover growth.

Counties Where Found:

Primarily below the Fall Line in the Coastal Plain, including (but not limited to): Mobile, Washington, Choctaw, Baldwin, Escambia, Conecuh, Covington, Coffee, Geneva, Houston, Henry, Barbour, and Pike Counties.

Nesting Habits & Movement

Movement & Burrow Use

Gopher tortoises are land-dwelling and spend up to 80% of their time within their burrows, which protect them from extreme temperatures, drought, fire, and predators.

  • Burrow Structure: Burrows are typically 15-20 feet long, sloping down to a chamber 6.5 feet deep, though some can reach 40 feet in length.
  • Locomotion: They move by walking on their elephantine hind legs and use their paddle-like forelimbs for digging and slow, determined walking. They cannot swim well.
  • Foraging: They forage for food (primarily grasses, forbs, and legumes) usually within 160 feet of their burrow entrance, using the burrow as a safe retreat.
  • Mating: Males travel farther than females during the breeding season (March to October) to find mates.

Nesting Habits

Females exhibit specific nesting behavior, relying on sunlit, sandy spots for successful incubation.

  • Nesting Season: Eggs are typically laid between May and July.
  • Clutch Size: One clutch per year, usually containing 5 to 9 eggs.
  • Nest Location: Nests are most often dug in the loose mound of sand at the burrow entrance, known as the burrow apron. This provides the soft, well-drained soil and sun exposure needed for incubation.

Footprints and Tracks for AI Tracking Models

For your AI tracking model, recognizing the distinct features of the Gopher Tortoise track is crucial. Since they prefer sandy, open habitats, their tracks are often found crossing sand roads or in the loose sand surrounding their burrows.

Track Characteristics:

  • Forefeet: Wide, short, and flattened, functioning like shovels. Tracks often show distinct, blunt claw marks.
  • Hindfeet: Elephantine and club-like, often leaving wide, round prints with less defined toe separation than the forefeet.
  • Trail: Due to their wide bodies and short legs, they leave a wide, somewhat wavering trail. The shell may sometimes drag a faint line down the center of the track path, especially in loose sand, though this is less common for an actively walking adult.
  • Distinguishing Feature: The presence of the large, half-moon shaped burrow entrance and its apron often confirms the tracks belong to a Gopher Tortoise.

Tip for Data Collection: Focus on collecting high-contrast images of tracks on fresh, moist sand to clearly define the edges and claw marks. This detail is essential for training computer vision models.
