The Flat-shelled Musk Turtle

(Sternotherus depressus) | High School AI Research

1. Species & Conservation Status

The Flat-shelled Musk Turtle is small, rarely exceeding 11 cm in carapace length, and is easily identified by its notably flattened upper shell (carapace). This adaptation allows it to hide deep within rock crevices.

STATUS: Endemic to Alabama and federally listed as a Threatened Species (ESA). The IUCN classifies it as Critically Endangered.

  • Scientific Name: Sternotherus depressus
  • Key Feature: Highly flattened carapace.
  • Primary Threat: Siltation and habitat degradation.

2. Habitat & Alabama Distribution

The Flat-shelled Musk Turtle is only found in one place in the world: the Upper Black Warrior River System in west-central Alabama.

Optimal Stream Habitat

  • Free-flowing creeks or small rivers.
  • Alternating deep pools (1-1.5m) and riffles.
  • Abundance of submerged boulders and crevices (for hiding).
  • Low silt load and minimal pollution.
  • Abundant food sources (snails and mussels).

3. Movement & Reproductive Habits

Movement & Activity

These turtles are **highly aquatic** and are generally poor swimmers. Their primary mode of movement is:

"WALKING ALONG THE RIVER BOTTOM"

  • Activity: Mostly nocturnal or crepuscular (dawn/dusk).
  • Basking: Rarely basks, remaining secretive and submerged.

Nesting Habits

Females have a low reproductive rate, laying small clutches annually:

  • Clutches per Year: Up to two clutches.
  • Nesting Season: May through early July.
  • Clutch Size: Small, typically 1 to 4 eggs.
  • Nest Site: Shallow nests dug in high sandy banks or the riparian zone (the area adjacent to the river) to avoid flooding.

4. Footprint Data for AI Tracking Model

Specific footprints for S. depressus are extremely rare. Use the general characteristics of a **Musk Turtle's gait** to train your model.

Key Tracking Signatures

  • Gait (Pattern): Turtles typically use an **"understep walk,"** where the hind foot lands behind the front foot. This creates two nearly continuous, parallel trails.
  • Appearance: The resulting pattern is often described as resembling a **"tank tread"** in soft sand or mud.
  • Foot/Toe Count: Tracks are generally oval in shape. Look for marks showing 5 toes on the front foot and typically 4-5 toes with prominent claw marks on the hind feet.
  • Drag Marks: Look for a thin, central groove caused by the tail dragging, or wider, intermittent marks from the edge of the shell when the turtle is resting or pushing off.

Visual Reference

Search near high, sandy banks close to the water for prints.
