Flattened Musk Turtle

(Sternotherus depressus)

Species Overview

  • Classification: Freshwater turtle, endemic to Alabama. Listed as *Threatened*.
  • Size: Small, rarely exceeding 11.0 cm (4.3 inches) in carapace length.
  • Physical Trait: The carapace (upper shell) is distinctly flat and low, an adaptation that allows it to quickly slip into narrow rock crevices for refuge and protection from strong currents.
  • Diet: Primarily carnivorous, feeding heavily on aquatic invertebrates, especially *freshwater snails* and introduced Asiatic clams.

Preferred Habitat

  • Type: Free-flowing large creeks and small rivers.
  • Structure: Optimal areas feature alternating pools (3.6 to 5 ft deep) and riffles, abundant submerged rocks, boulders, and crevices.
  • Water Quality: Requires **low silt load** and minimal pollution, as siltation fills the vital rock crevices the turtle uses for cover and eliminates its primary snail food source.

Distribution in Alabama (Endemic)

The Flattened Musk Turtle is **endemic** (only found) to the state of Alabama, specifically within the upper **Black Warrior River system** above the Bankhead Dam.

  • Blount County
  • Cullman County
  • Etowah County
  • Fayette County
  • Jefferson County
  • Lawrence County
  • Marion County
  • Marshall County
  • Tuscaloosa County
  • Walker County
  • Winston County

Nesting Habits

  • Nesting Season: May through September.
  • Clutch Size: Small, typically 1 to 3 eggs per clutch. Females lay two clutches per year.
  • Nest Location: Females briefly leave the water to deposit eggs in shallow nests. Nests are typically dug in high sandy banks or within the surrounding riparian zone (river bank), usually within 100 feet of the water in full or partial sun areas.

Movement and Behavior

  • Activity: Primarily **Nocturnal** for adults, especially during warm summer months (April to October). Juveniles are more likely to be active during the day.
  • Home Range: Adults exhibit small home ranges, averaging about 332 m in stream length. They are bottom-dwellers and rarely move far from their preferred rock crevice refugia.
  • Increased Movement: Movement increases during the breeding/nesting season (Spring/Early Summer) and is positively correlated with water temperature and precipitation.

Footprint Data for Tracking Model

**Key Insight:** This species is highly aquatic and spends most of its life on the rocky/cobble stream bed, rarely basking or moving far on land.

Due to its life history, clear, terrestrial footprint tracks are **rare** and challenging to find, except for the short distance traveled by nesting females or hatchlings moving to the water.

  • Foot Structure: Musk turtles have five claws on each foot and are partially webbed.
  • Track Profile: When moving on soft substrate (sand/mud near nest sites), tracks would likely show alternating *claw marks* (three to five per foot) and a partial or faint *webbing impression*, often with a drag line from the tail or posterior carapace.
  • AI Model Focus: For this species, the AI model should be trained to look for track patterns near *sandy riparian zones* during nesting season (May-Sept), or possibly use **acoustic/visual sensors** to track movement underwater, as terrestrial footprints are not the primary data source.
